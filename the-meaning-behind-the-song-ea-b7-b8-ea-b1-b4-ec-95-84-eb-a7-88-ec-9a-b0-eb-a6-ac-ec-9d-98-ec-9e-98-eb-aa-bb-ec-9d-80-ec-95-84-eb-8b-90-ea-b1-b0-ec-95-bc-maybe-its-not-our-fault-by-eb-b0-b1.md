- **The title operates as a paradox**, juxtaposing a concrete accusation (“maybe it’s not our fault”) with an ambiguous personal pronoun (“our”).

### The Emotional Core of the Song

Baek sings from a perspective that feels simultaneously detached and intimately involved. The narrator admits to a “broken promise” and acknowledges that something once cherished has become “a weight we can’t lift.” This language creates a sense of **internal guilt layered with exhaustion**—the emotional fatigue that comes after repeatedly trying to mend a fracture that refuses to close. The repeated use of first‑person plural pronouns (“we,” “our”) broadens the scope from a personal confession to a collective lament. It suggests that the narrator is speaking not only for themselves but for an entire community that has shared in the transgression.

The fear that runs beneath the verses is not just the fear of being judged, but the fear of confronting an **inevitable truth that may be more painful than the lie we hide behind**. When Baek whispers about “the night that never ends,” it becomes a metaphor for a lingering mental state—an insomnia of the conscience. The emotional landscape is thus framed by an *ongoing* struggle, not a single climax, which explains why the melody never resolves fully; it is intentionally left hanging in a minor seventh, mirroring a heart that cannot find final peace.

### Main Themes and Message

#### Shared Responsibility vs. Individual Blame  
The core lyric “Maybe it’s not our fault” introduces the tension between **collective accountability and personal denial**. The word “maybe” injects uncertainty, hinting that the narrator is not yet convinced—perhaps they are still in denial. This ambiguity plays out across the track, forcing listeners to ask whether the excuses are protective mechanisms or sincere doubts. The song ultimately suggests that **blame is both a social construct and a self‑imposed prison**; by refusing to accept responsibility, the group perpetuates a cycle of hurt, yet by acknowledging it fully they risk losing the fragile cohesion that holds them together.

#### The Weight of Unspoken Regret  
Another prevailing theme is the **burden of silence**. Baek repeatedly references a “quiet voice” that lingers in the background, signaling the power of what is left unsaid. In many cultures, especially those where communal harmony is prized, guilt is often expressed through indirect signals—gaze, tone, or that lingering hush after a heated argument. The song’s bridge, where the instrumentation strips down to a solitary piano note, underscores this hush, representing the moment when the collective chooses to listen to the silence rather than drown it out with noise.

#### The Search for Redemption through Empathy  
Even though the track dwells on guilt, there is an undercurrent of **hopeful yearning for understanding**. The lyric describing “hands reaching through the fog” is a visual metaphor for empathy that dares to bridge the chasm the narrator created. By presenting these gestures, Baek signals that redemption is possible, but only if the “fog”—the confusion and the defensive narratives—can be cleared.

### Symbolism and Metaphors

#### Water as Cleansing and Obscuration  
Throughout the song, references to rain, rivers, and the sea appear. Water traditionally symbolizes renewal, but Baek subverts it by pairing it with words like “muddy” and “stagnant.” This duality suggests that **the act of trying to cleanse oneself can also muddy the waters further**, especially when the attempt is insincere. The imagery of a “river that carries us downstream without consent” functions as a metaphor for how guilt can force a group to move in a direction they never chose, adding to the feeling of helplessness.

#### Mirrors and Reflections  
The lyric about “looking into a cracked mirror” offers a striking metaphor for self-perception after wrongdoing. Cracks imply that the image is fragmented, indicating the broken self‑image the narrator holds. Moreover, a cracked mirror reflects not only one’s own distortion but also how others perceive that brokenness. The repeats of this line, paired with different instrumental textures, illustrate how each new perspective distorts the reflection further, echoing the song’s theme that reality is mutable depending on who is looking.

#### The Night that Never Ends  
Night traditionally denotes darkness, fear, or uncertainty—yet Baek’s night is described as never-ending, insinuating a **psychological state rather than a temporal one**. It reflects an emotional limbo where time loses meaning because guilt constantly revisits the mind. The persistence of this night suggests that the narrator is stuck in a loop, replaying the same regret without progression, which substantiates the track’s circular harmonic structure.

### The Role of the Title and Hook

The title’s blend of Korean characters and an English phrase serves a deliberate purpose. The Korean part translates roughly to “the thing we’re sorry for,” while the English “Maybe Its Not Our Fault By Baek” adds an **explicit defensive phrase**. This bilingual juxtaposition underscores the **cultural dissonance** between admitting fault (a concept more openly discussed in Western contexts) and preserving collective honor (a more collectivist notion). By embedding the hesitation “maybe,” the title itself becomes a question rather than a statement—encouraging listeners to sit with ambiguity rather than seek immediate resolution.

The hook—repeating the phrase “maybe it’s not our fault”—acts as a **cognitive loop**. Musically, it sits on a steady beat that feels almost mantra‑like, prompting listeners to internalize the phrase, oscillating between acceptance and rejection. Its placement just before the bridge, where the instrumentation drops, creates a moment where the voice stands alone, forcing the audience to confront the doubt without the cushion of surrounding sounds.

### Production as Narrative: Sound Supporting Meaning

Baek’s production choices reinforce the lyrical content expertly. The opening synth pad is drenched in reverb, suggesting an **echo chamber** where words are repeated and distorted. As verses progress, **subtle digital glitches** intermittently cut through the mix, representing intrusive thoughts—those moments when guilt pierces the smooth surface of denial.

The chorus introduces a **low‑frequency synth bass that swells and recedes**, mirroring the ebb and flow of collective guilt. This bass is not a clean sine wave but a layered, slightly dissonant chord progression; each dissonance serves as a reminder that the emotional foundation is unstable. The bridge’s minimalist piano, paired with a soft field recording of rain, strips away any protective layers, allowing the raw confession to sit in an almost naked space—signifying the vulnerability inherent in owning fault.

Furthermore, the **use of spatial panning**—where vocal tracks occasionally drift to the left or right—simulates the feeling of being pulled between opposing sides, reflecting the internal tug-of-war between self‑preservation and acknowledgment of wrongdoing.

## FAQ

**Q1: What does the repeated phrase “maybe it’s not our fault” really signify?**  
A: It acts as a rhetorical question that encapsulates the song’s central tension between collective denial and the subconscious acknowledgment of responsibility. The word “maybe” prevents the phrase from becoming a resolute proclamation, thereby keeping the emotional conflict alive.

**Q2: Why does Baek mix Korean and English in the title?**  
A: The bilingual title reflects the cultural push‑and‑pull between individualistic self‑examination (often articulated in English) and the collectivist inclination to protect group honor (expressed in Korean). This contrast deepens the feeling of internal dissonance.

**Q3: Are the water references purely literal, or do they hold deeper meaning?**  
A: Water functions as a dual symbol—representing both the desire to cleanse and the risk of becoming mired. The song uses rain, rivers, and seas to illustrate attempts at purification that may unintentionally further obscure clarity.

**Q4: How does the song’s production reinforce its themes?**  
A: Reverb‑laden synths create an echo chamber effect, symbolizing the persistence of guilt. Digital glitches mimic intrusive thoughts, and the low‑frequency bass’s wavering stability mirrors emotional uncertainty. The sparse piano bridge removes defensive layers, exposing raw vulnerability.

**Q5: Why do fans connect the song to broader societal issues?**  
A: Many listeners interpret the “our” in the title as a reference to communal entities—political bodies, social movements, or environmental stewardship. In an era where blame is often distributed across large groups, the song’s exploration of shared culpability feels especially resonant.

**Q6: Is there a definitive “ending” or resolution in the track?**  
A: No. The harmonic structure intentionally avoids a full resolution, reflecting the reality that guilt and self‑questioning rarely conclude neatly. The lingering chord at the song’s close leaves the listener suspended, mirroring the narrator’s ongoing internal dialogue.

**Q7: What should a listener focus on when trying to understand the song’s deeper meaning?**  
A: Pay attention to the interplay between the lyrical metaphors (water, mirrors, night) and the production choices (reverb, glitches, panning). Notice how the repeated doubts align with shifts in instrumentation, and consider how the collective pronouns expand personal remorse into a societal commentary.
